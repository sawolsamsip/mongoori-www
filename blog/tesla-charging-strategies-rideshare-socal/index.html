Optimizing Home Charging Schedules

The overnight charging protocol for maximum profit:

  1. 9 PM-6 AM off-peak window: Charge to only 80% overnight

    • Why 80%? Tesla data shows 80-20% charging extends battery lifespan by 40% vs. 0-100% daily
    • 80% = 240-250 miles of range (sufficient for 1-2 full days of rideshare)
    • Energy cost: ~$4.80 (40 kWh × $0.12/kWh off-peak rate)
    • Charging duration: 6-7 hours (complete by 3-4 AM; time for additional charges if needed)

  2. Schedule charging to start 30 min before peak rate ends:

    • Use Tesla app to set "Scheduled Charging" for 8:30 PM start
    • This ensures most charging happens during off-peak 9 PM-6 AM window
    • Final 10-20% tops off during morning partial-peak, minimizing premium rate exposure

  3. Summer optimization (June-September peak rates):

    • Reduce to 60-70% overnight (saves $1.20/charge)
    • Add mid-week top-ups on low-demand days if available
    • Avoid charging during 12 PM-6 PM summer peak (saves $0.10+/kWh)

Monthly savings from optimized home charging vs. default 100% charging:

  • Reduced charging time optimization: ~$15-20/month
  • Reduced summer peak exposure: ~$10-15/month
  • Battery longevity value (40% lifespan extension): ~$25-40/month (depreciation savings)
  • Total: $50-75/month = $600-900/year

Home Charging + Time-of-Use Rate Plans

If you don't have a TOU rate plan, request one from your utility:

California Edison, Southern California Gas Company, and other utilities offer:

  • Residential time-of-use plans (no extra cost to switch)
  • Typically 20-40% cheaper during off-peak hours
  • Perfect for EV owners who charge at night

Impact: Switching to TOU from standard residential rates saves $40-80/month for EV charging alone

4. Supercharging: When, Where & How to Minimize Costs

Understanding Supercharger Costs

California Supercharger pricing (2026):

  • Idle fee: $0.50/minute after charge completes (severe penalty)
  • Charging rate: $0.28-0.42/kWh (varies by location and time)
  • Average 10%-80% charge cost: $8-12 (230 kW input at $0.35/kWh average)
  • Average charge time: 25-30 minutes

When to Supercharge:

  • Emergency (low battery, long distance requirement)
  • During surge pricing (if extra revenue covers energy cost)
  • If you can combine it with a required break

When NOT to Supercharge:

  • Daily home charging is available
  • If doing short local rideshare (under 200 miles)
  • During off-peak hours (use home charging instead)

Strategic Supercharger Use for Profitability

The "surge timing" strategy:

  1. Evening surge (5 PM-7 PM): High ride demand, high prices ($2+/mile possible)
  2. Pre-surge Supercharge (4:30-5 PM off-peak): Charge to 90% while rates are still low
  3. Surge rideshare (5 PM-7:30 PM): Fresh battery + peak demand = highest profit hours
  4. Example math:
    • Charge cost at 4:30 PM: $10 (10%-80% charge)
    • Surge rideshare revenue: 60 miles × $2.00/mile = $120
    • Non-surge would have been: 60 miles × $1.15/mile = $69
    • Revenue difference: $51 (covers $10 charge + $41 profit gain)

Supercharger Etiquette & Idle Fee Avoidance

Critical rule: Don't let your car sit at Supercharger after charging completes

  • Idle fee: $0.50/minute after charge complete (can add $5-10 to your session)
  • Set phone timer for when charge should complete (typically 25-30 min for 10-80%)
  • Be ready to move car immediately (have next destination ready)
  • Or use Scheduled Charging at home instead (no penalties)

7. Battery Health Optimization: Charging Practices That Extend Vehicle Lifespan

Understanding Tesla Battery Degradation

Modern Tesla batteries are remarkably durable—but your charging habits determine longevity:

Battery health factors (impact on degradation):

Factor Impact Your Control
Charging to 100% daily -0.5% annual High
Fast charging >80% -0.3% annual Medium
Heat during charging -0.2% annual Low
Time at high SOC -0.3% annual High
Thermal management +0.2% longevity benefit Medium

Real-world degradation rates:

  • Optimal charging (60-80%, off-peak): ~2% per 100K miles
  • Average rideshare (0-100% mixed): ~4-5% per 100K miles
  • Poor charging (100% constantly, fast charge): ~6-7% per 100K miles

Financial impact over 150,000 miles:

  • Optimal strategy: -3% capacity = -10-12 kWh = lose 30-40 miles range
  • Average rideshare: -6% capacity = -20-25 kWh = lose 60-80 miles range
  • Poor strategy: -10% capacity = -33-40 kWh = lose 100-130 miles range

Dollar value of extended battery life:

  • Tesla battery replacement cost: $10,000-15,000 per pack
  • Each 1% additional capacity retention: Worth ~$100-150 in used car value
  • Optimal charging practices preserve $1,500-2,000 in residual vehicle value

Battery Health Best Practices for Rideshare Drivers

Golden Rules:

  1. Keep daily charge between 20-80% (Sweet spot for battery longevity)

    • Overnight charge: 20%-80% target (vs. 0%-100%)
    • Never let battery drop below 5% (hard on cells)
    • Never charge to 100% unless long road trip (save 100% for emergencies only)

  2. Charge during cool hours (avoid heat stress)

    • Southern California summer: Charge 9 PM-6 AM (avoid hot afternoons)
    • Use Scheduled Charging to avoid noon-3 PM peak heat
    • Winter: Less critical, can charge anytime

  3. Use moderate charging speeds when possible

    • Home charging (11.5 kW): Ideal, gentlest on battery
    • Supercharging (up to 250 kW): Acceptable when necessary, but not daily
    • Level 1 (1.4 kW): Too slow for rideshare needs

  4. Keep battery temp optimal

    • Precondition battery before charging (Tesla app: Climate → Heat Battery, -5-10 min)
    • This warms battery to optimal charge temperature
    • Especially important for winter/cold-weather charging

9. Seasonal Charging Optimization

Winter Charging Strategy (November-February)

Challenges:

  • Cold reduces battery efficiency by 20-30%
  • Range drops 30-40% in winter vs. summer
  • Charging speed slower in cold
  • Heating battery before charging is mandatory

Winter protocol:

  • Home charge to 80% becomes "target 90%" (to compensate for cold loss)
  • Charge on 9 PM-6 AM schedule (still off-peak)
  • Use Scheduled Charging + Preconditioning
    • Settings: Climate → "Heat Battery" enabled
    • Charge starts at 8 PM, preheats until 9 PM, charges 9 PM-6 AM
  • Plan for 20-25% lower range (200-mile car → 150-160 miles in winter)

Winter cost impact:

  • Slightly higher home charging (heat battery uses ~2-3 kWh preheating): +$0.50/charge
  • Fewer Supercharger visits (plan around range loss)
  • Net winter cost increase: ~$15-25/month

Summer Charging Strategy (June-September)

Advantages:

  • Peak electricity demand = Peak electricity rates
  • BUT also peak rideshare demand (tourism, heat-driven short trips)
  • Summer surge pricing opportunities

Summer protocol:

  • Reduce home charge to 60-70% (saves energy on hot charges)
  • Charge specifically during 9 PM-midnight window (before hardest peak 12 AM-6 AM rises)
  • Avoid charging between 12 PM-9 PM (highest rates: $0.35-0.42/kWh)
  • Plan Supercharging for early morning (5-6 AM, off-peak rates) vs. afternoon

Summer cost advantage:

  • Reduced home charging (60% vs. 80%): -$1.20/charge
  • Avoided peak-hour charging: -$15-20/month
  • Net summer cost savings: $20-35/month

14. Avoiding Common Charging Mistakes

Mistake 1: Charging to 100% Daily

Cost: -1% extra battery degradation per year

  • Over 150K miles: -1.5% capacity = loss of 5-8 kWh = 15-25 miles range
  • Financial impact: -$150-250 in used vehicle value
  • Plus: Slower charging from 80-100% (takes 2x longer for 20% gain)

Fix: Charge to 80% for daily driving, 100% only for road trips

Mistake 2: Using Supercharger Instead of Home Charging

Cost: 6-8x more expensive

  • Supercharger: $10-12 per full charge
  • Home charge: $1.50-2 per full charge
  • Daily difference: $8-10 = $240-300/month

Fix: Make home charging your primary method; Supercharge only for emergencies

Mistake 3: Letting Idle Fees Accumulate at Superchargers

Cost: $0.50/minute after charging finishes

  • Lingering 20 minutes at Supercharger: +$10 charge
  • 2-3x per month: -$20-30/month

Fix: Set phone timer, move immediately when charge hits 80%

Mistake 4: Not Taking Advantage of Free Destination Charging

Cost: Missing $5-10/month in free energy

  • 10 destination charges/month × $1.50 average value = $15/month
  • Over year: $180 opportunity cost

Fix: Route rides to include stops at destination chargers

Mistake 5: Charging During Peak Electricity Hours

Cost: 2-3x higher electricity rates

  • Peak rate ($0.40/kWh) vs. off-peak ($0.13/kWh)
  • Single charge difference: $2-5/charge
  • 10 accidental peak charges/month: -$20-50/month

Fix: Set scheduled charging for off-peak hours only (9 PM-6 AM)

15. Charging & Passenger Experience

Do Passengers Care About Your Charging Strategy?

Generally: No. But charging timing affects experience:

What passengers notice:

  • Smooth acceleration (good for profitability)
  • Climate control (affects comfort)
  • Overall range/reliability (affects ratings)

What passengers don't care about:

  • Whether you charged at home vs. Supercharger
  • Your electricity cost
  • Your battery health strategy

Implication: You optimize charging for profit, not passenger appeal.

When Charging Affects Passenger Pickup

Scenario: You're at Supercharger when passenger requests ride

  • Passenger requests at 4 PM
  • You're mid-charge, 15 minutes remaining
  • Passenger is 8 minutes away, will wait

Options:

  1. Cancel charge, serve passenger immediately
  2. Continue charge (risk passenger cancelling)
  3. Move car, serve passenger, return to charge later

Financial decision:

  • Lose $8 in Supercharge completion → Serve $15 ride = net $7 gain
  • Always serve the passenger; you can charge later

Best practice: Check ride requests before arriving at Supercharger; time your charges during slow hours (2-4 PM) when ride requests are lowest

FAQ

Q: How much does it cost to charge a Tesla at home? A: For a Model 3, full charge (0-100%) costs $4-5 on residential rates, or $2-3 on TOU off-peak rates. A 80% charge (typical daily) costs $3-4 off-peak.

Q: Can I use Supercharger daily for rideshare? A: Technically yes, but it's 5-6x more expensive than home charging and wears your battery faster. Home charging should be primary (at least 80% of charges).

Q: How long does overnight home charging take? A: Wall Connector charges at 30-44 miles/hour, so 80% charge takes 6-8 hours, completing by 3-4 AM if you start at 9 PM.

Q: Are destination chargers really free? A: Yes—if you're already a customer of that restaurant/retailer. It's included as customer amenity, not a separate charge.

Q: Should I charge while picking up passengers? A: No—unless you're using a destination charger while a passenger is shopping/dining. For active rideshare, charging disrupts profitability.

Q: Does cold weather affect charging? A: Yes, winter reduces charging efficiency by 15-20% and extends charging time. Plan for longer charge durations in winter.

Q: Can I charge to 100% without damaging the battery? A: Occasionally, yes (for road trips). Regularly: No. It reduces battery lifespan by 40% vs. 80% max charging.
